Good morning, it's Sunday, 1 March 2026, and I'm bringing you four major stories to kick off your week. 

The biggest story this morning is Apollo Global Management's £4.2bn acquisition of a major UK-listed infrastructure investment platform, marking one of the largest UK take-privates since the rate environment stabilised. The deal involves a sovereign wealth co-investor and is expected to complete by Q3, subject to FCA and CMA clearance. Advisers on the deal include A&O Shearman for Apollo and Freshfields for the target board, with Lazard and Goldman running the M&A process. The implications for law firms are significant, with both A&O Shearman and Freshfields facing an intensive regulatory advisory phase due to the CMA's heightened scrutiny of infrastructure acquisitions, particularly post-Rosebank. Meanwhile, Kirkland & Ellis is likely to be advising Apollo on the fund structuring and LP capital calls, and Latham & Watkins will likely be leading on the leveraged acquisition financing.

Turning now to the capital markets, Revolut has formally engaged Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley as global coordinators for a proposed London listing that would value the company at approximately £8bn. The float would mark one of London's most significant tech listings in years, and Linklaters and Clifford Chance are the natural leads for the issuer-side mandate. They will be handling the prospectus liability work, FCA sponsor obligations, and structuring of Revolut's dual-class share architecture. Across the Atlantic, Sullivan & Cromwell and Davis Polk are likely to be advising Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley, and will be working on the underwriting agreement and negotiating indemnity provisions.

Meanwhile, on the regulatory front, the EU AI Act enforcement has entered Phase 2, with the EU AI Office issuing its first binding decisions under the new rules. Two US-headquartered AI systems have been found to have constituted prohibited biometric categorisation, and face maximum fines of €35m or 7% of global annual turnover. Clifford Chance's technology, media and telecommunications practice is well-positioned to advise US tech firms on their AI Act compliance posture, while Linklaters' cross-border regulatory capability positions it for multi-jurisdictional mandates covering both AI Act and GDPR intersection work.

In other news, BP has agreed to sell a package of mature North Sea producing assets to Harbour Energy for £1.4bn in cash, as part of its revised strategy to reduce upstream exposure and redirect capital to its gas and low-carbon transition portfolio. Herbert Smith Freehills is advising Harbour Energy, while Linklaters is advising BP. This deal reinforces Herbert Smith Freehills' pre-eminent North Sea energy M&A practice and creates substantial additional advisory work around decommissioning liability mechanics.

Sector Watch: Private equity dealmaking is showing its clearest signs of structural recovery since rates peaked. LP distributions have improved, credit markets have reopened at workable margins, and the bid-ask gap that paralysed M&A in 2023–2024 is compressing. The watch this week is whether improved sentiment converts into completed transactions. One to Follow: The CMA's formal investigation into cloud computing market concentration, which could impose structural remedies on hyperscaler providers and has direct implications for technology M&A and commercial contracts across every sector.
