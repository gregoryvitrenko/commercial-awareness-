The biggest story this morning is a landmark deal in private markets. EQT, the Swedish-listed private equity giant, has acquired Coller Capital, one of the world's leading secondaries specialists, in a transaction that brings nearly fifty billion dollars of assets under management onto EQT's platform. No deal value has been disclosed, but the strategic logic is hard to miss. Secondaries dry powder is at record levels globally, GP-led continuation funds are expected to have a record-breaking year, and EQT has just bought itself a structural advantage in exactly that market. For law firms, this is a significant mandate — Kirkland and Ellis dominates European PE fund-level M&A and regularly advises EQT, while Linklaters and Freshfields would be natural candidates given their funds practices and Nordic relationships. And with Coller holding UK-regulated entities, FCA change-of-control consent will be required — that's specialist regulatory counsel work that firms like Slaughter and May are well placed to handle.

Turning now to the capital markets, and a meaningful vote of confidence in London. Valterra Platinum, the platinum group metals company spun out of Anglo American, has completed a seven-point-five billion pound listing on the London Stock Exchange's Main Market, with Macfarlanes confirmed as adviser. This is one of the most significant primary market events on the LSE in recent memory, and it arrives as the FCA's reformed listing regime continues to bed in. Under the new rules, issuers must make a single application to list all securities of the relevant class — a structural simplification that the Valterra transaction is now putting into practice at scale. The City of London Law Society also published revised Takeover Code specimen documents just last week, on the 26th of February, meaning capital markets counsel on this deal were working against a very live documentation update. For Magic Circle firms, Freshfields and Linklaters are the dominant players for FTSE-level equity capital markets work. US firms like Sullivan and Cromwell may act for underwriters or institutional investors, but the primary listing and UKLA-facing work stays firmly with UK-qualified counsel.

Meanwhile, on the banking and finance front, a notable tension is emerging in private credit. GlobalCapital reported this week that private credit players are pushing back after Blue Owl Capital cast doubt on the sector's near-term expansion prospects — a sign of real strategic divergence among direct lenders about whether the asset class is approaching saturation or still in a growth phase. At the same time, MUFG has hired from Crédit Agricole to build out its leveraged finance bench in London, signalling that institutional banks are investing in origination capability to compete with private credit funds for the same sponsor-backed paper. A Citizens Financial survey found that fifty-eight percent of middle-market executives expect M&A volumes to rise in 2026, which is the pipeline signal that leveraged finance desks are positioning around. Clifford Chance and Allen and Overy Shearman have the deepest leveraged finance practices in the Magic Circle, while Kirkland and Latham dominate the US-sponsor side of the market in London.

Across the Atlantic, the White House announced yesterday that major technology companies have signed a voluntary pledge to self-fund the power costs of their AI data centres, with President Trump framing it as protection for consumer electricity bills. The pledge is voluntary, with no enforcement mechanism publicly described — a deliberate contrast with the EU's binding AI Act and Energy Efficiency Directive. But voluntary or not, the transactional consequences are real. Companies that have committed to covering their own power costs will need to execute power purchase agreements, grid connection contracts, on-site generation structures, and battery storage arrangements at pace. Latham and Watkins has the deepest US bench for energy project finance and power purchase agreements. Clifford Chance and Linklaters, with their global energy and infrastructure practices, are well-placed for the cross-border structuring work this will generate.

On the regulatory front, two developments out of Brussels. The European Commission has closed a dominance probe into a medical device manufacturer without finding an infringement — consistent with a broader pattern of the Commission prioritising enforcement resources on cases with the greatest economic impact. More significantly, the Commission has escalated a formal investigation under the Foreign Subsidies Regulation into Goldwind, the Chinese wind turbine maker active in the EU through its subsidiary Vensys. This is only the second FSR investigation focused on a Chinese entity, and it confirms the regulation is becoming an active enforcement mechanism. The FSR gives the Commission powers to investigate state financial contributions and impose remedies including divestiture. Cleary Gottlieb Brussels is the leading firm on novel EU enforcement, while Linklaters and Freshfields have dedicated foreign subsidies teams built precisely for this moment.

In disputes, a landmark ruling from the UK High Court. In Mariana v BHP Group, the court has found BHP liable under Brazilian law for the 2015 collapse of the Fundão tailings dam — described as the worst environmental disaster in Brazil's history. The judgment confirms that English courts are prepared to apply foreign law to hold a UK-domiciled parent liable for overseas subsidiary conduct. The quantum phase — determining the actual financial exposure — is still to come, and given the scale of the harm, that number could be very large. Herbert Smith Freehills has one of the strongest international litigation and mining sector practices in the UK, and this ruling will be studied closely by every energy and resources company with a UK parent and overseas operations.

And finally, in AI law, the Department of Justice's AI Litigation Task Force, established in January under Attorney General Pam Bondi, is preparing to challenge state AI laws in federal court — but it's waiting on the Department of Commerce to identify specific targets first. The other date to have in your diary is the eleventh of March, next Wednesday, when the FTC must publish its policy statement on how Section 5 of the FTC Act applies to AI models. Together, these two developments are shaping the federal AI regulatory framework under Trump: pre-empt the states, and apply a narrowly framed consumer protection overlay. For firms advising technology clients, the compliance window between state law proliferation and federal pre-emption is exactly where the advisory work sits right now.
